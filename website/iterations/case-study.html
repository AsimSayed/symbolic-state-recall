Case Study

We Are All Blind

HCDE 515 · University of Washington Winter 2026 Instructor: Prof. Leah Findlater

Sighted students glance at their work to recover state. Blind students navigate line-by-line through every character to find a single term, shattering their flow. We partnered with a blind computer science student to find out what's actually going wrong — and to design something better.

Design Partner

Abdallah Altahaineh

Abdallah is a Computer Science student at the University of Washington from Dubai. He has near-total blindness — he can perceive large changes in light and shadow, silhouettes of buildings, but no detail. He navigates the world and his coursework almost entirely through his screen reader and his memory.

He relies on VoiceOver across his Apple ecosystem — MacBook, iPhone, iPad. He uses Pages and Notes for math, VS Code for coding, and has memorized the full keyboard layout including every special character. He walks 7,000 to 10,000 steps a day, plans his own camping trips, and navigates campus without a cane. When asked how he walks in a straight line: "I hope. That's very much what I can say."

We worked with Abdallah across three in-person sessions at Maple Hall over the course of the quarter — a need-finding interview, a co-design session where we watched him solve real calculus problems, and a final session where he tested our prototype.

The Problem

The barrier is memory, not comprehension

We went in expecting to find problems with math notation, screen reader pronunciation, or equation formatting. We were wrong. Abdallah is strong at math. He's strong at STEM in general. The problem is far more fundamental than we expected.

Sighted students use the screen as persistent external working memory. When you're solving a multi-step equation, you glance up at the original expression, check a coefficient, and keep writing. You don't even think about it. It takes less than a second.

For Abdallah, that "glance" becomes a full navigation event. He has to stop writing, arrow up through his document line-by-line, listen to content character-by-character until he finds what he needs, hold it in his head, navigate back to his insertion point, and resume. Every time he needs to check a value, the cycle repeats. And every cycle costs him his place in the problem.

“I keep having to scroll up to reread the original equation and copy it down. Sighted students can glance and keep typing. I have to keep navigating back and forth.”
— Abdallah, need-finding session

This creates what we came to call a cognitive load ceiling. The intrinsic complexity of the math — the actual thinking — gets compounded by the extraneous burden of mental state tracking. The harder the problem, the more intermediate values there are to hold, and the more navigations break his flow.

What we observed

Working memory is the bottleneck, not reasoning

Abdallah never struggled with the math itself. He struggled with holding intermediate values in memory while simultaneously reasoning through transformations. As he put it: "Working memory is the worst."

The scribe is a memory device, not a solver

During co-design, we gave Abdallah a human scribe. He never asked the scribe to solve anything. He asked them to recall: "Can you remind me what the equation was?", "What was the first term?", "Can you read what I have so far?" He was focused on doing the work. He just needed someone to hold state for him.

Clarification and recall are distinct needs

Abdallah drew a clear line: "Memory is when I completely forgot something. Clarifying is when I'm 90% sure but want to check." Two different interaction patterns, two different levels of urgency.

Mental retracing catches errors

Without visual scanning, Abdallah catches mistakes by mentally replaying his steps. When asked how he knew he'd forgotten something: "I retraced the steps in my head." Effective, but cognitively expensive.

“Math is mostly understanding and not memorizing. No one is memorizing when they're solving math. They're solving something.”
— Abdallah, co-design session
Competitive Analysis

What already exists — and what's missing

We analyzed tools across the memory-offloading space: BrailleNote for braille-based notetaking, Plaud NotePin for wearable audio capture, Meta AI Glasses for hands-free descriptions, Quip for clipboard management. Each handles one piece of the problem — capture, or retrieval, or structured storage — but none of them connect these for cognitively demanding, real-time tasks like solving equations.

The gap is clear: effective memory offloading requires low-friction capture, structured indexing, and rapid non-linear retrieval. Existing tools fragment these across domains and none are built for the specific cognitive demands of blind users doing STEM work.

Co-Design

Abdallah solves calculus. We watch and learn.

In our second session, we brought real math problems — implicit differentiation, related rates — and watched Abdallah work through them, first alone and then with a human scribe. The difference was immediate. With the scribe holding state, Abdallah could reason continuously without the scroll-listen-navigate-scroll cycle breaking his concentration.

But the most important moment came during brainstorming. I proposed a concept: a tool that would parse equations into structured trees, index every term, and let you summon any piece back to your cursor with a keyboard shortcut. Abdallah didn't just agree — he refined it on the spot:

“It could be a program that looks like a normal document but stores what I write in structured maps. If I type 2x + 3 = 9, the program automatically identifies left and right sides, breaks the left side into terms, stores them as keys and values. Then I could press a shortcut like 'Left 1' to retrieve 2x, 'Left 2' for 3, and 'Right' for 9.”
— Abdallah, co-design session, elaborating on the proposed concept

He went further: "It should understand nested structures — numerator, denominator, inside parentheses — and store those too." And then: "I could actually code something like this. I'm planning to build an accessible study tool this summer."

Design Principles

Six principles from the research

These emerged from coding our session transcripts, mapping Abdallah's pain points, and testing our assumptions against his actual workflow.

1

Don't interrupt the current context to retrieve prior information

Document navigation is a cognitive cost. Retrieval should not require scrolling, mode-switching, or cursor relocation.

2

Don't disrupt cognitive flow for recall or verification

Recalling intermediate values should not reset screen reader focus or require changing reading modes. Support real-time state recall while preserving cursor position.

3

Preserve user agency in logical reasoning

The system provides memory scaffolding, not automated solving. It should never complete transformations or solve steps on behalf of the user.

4

Preserve structural and semantic integrity of notation

The system recognizes domain-specific notation — "B'" not "B Prime," "^2" not "rectangle" — and maintains mathematical structure for recall.

5

Operate within existing infrastructure

Integrate into established accessibility ecosystems — VoiceOver, macOS, Pages, Notes — rather than replacing workflows the user already trusts.

6

Keep intermediate mental steps persistently available

Multi-step tasks generate meaningful but short-lived intermediate states. The system stabilizes and indexes these so they can be recalled without mental reconstruction.

Design Concepts

Three directions explored

We developed three design concepts, each addressing the core problem through a different modality. All three went through critique with Abdallah and our course instructors.

Symbolic State Recall via Hotkeys Built

A keyboard-driven system that parses equations into a navigable tree and lets users summon any term with a compact query grammar. No new symbols needed — just numbers and L/R. This became Math Recall.

Haptic Syntax Ring Explored

A wearable ring that translates mathematical structure hierarchy into tactile feedback — pressure and vibration patterns encoding nesting depth, with physical gestures for save and replay.

AI Voice Scribe Explored

A virtual AI companion running alongside existing tools, providing conversational voice-based retrieval of previously written steps. "What was the value of x?"

We chose the hotkey-based recall system because it most directly addressed the core need — structured random-access recall without leaving the insertion point. It aligned with what Abdallah himself had described during brainstorming, and it could integrate into his existing macOS workflow without requiring new hardware or AI dependencies.

The Result

Testing the prototype

We built a working prototype in Swift — a command-line tool demonstrating the recall grammar, AST parsing, tree navigation, and spoken feedback loop. In our final session, Abdallah tested it with both single-line and multi-line equations.

The query grammar works like this: you press Option+Space to enter recall mode, then type a path — 1 L 1 for the first term on the left side of line 1, 1 R 1 for the first term on the right. After every token, the system speaks what it found. You hear "Line 1", "Left side, 2 items", "x squared" as you navigate deeper. Press Space to insert the selected term at your cursor. Backspace retraces one level up. Escape exits.

Fractions, integrals, powers, and roots all expand into child nodes you can drill into. The grammar scales to any depth without new commands.

“If a human scribe is a 10, this tool is like a 9 or 9.5.”
— Abdallah, after testing the prototype

He liked the left/right organization, term-by-term navigation, and how fractions opened into numerator and denominator child nodes. He suggested improvements: seamless switching between typing and recall mode, lateral navigation between sides without backtracking, and extending the system to multi-file coding tasks.

Critically, he distinguished between tool confusion and task confusion: "What's confusing me is not the steps we can track — what's confusing me is what the question itself wants us to do." The recall mechanism was clear. The math was the hard part — as it should be.

What's Next

From prototype to product

The CLI prototype validated the interaction model. Now Math Recall is becoming a native macOS app — built with Swift and AppKit, running as a background service that intercepts the keyboard trigger, communicates with VoiceOver through NSAccessibility protocols, and inserts text directly into the active text field.

It won't feel like a separate app. It will feel like a native layer woven into the screen reader experience — invisible until you need it, instant when you do.

Math Recall doesn't make math easier. It makes math fair.
